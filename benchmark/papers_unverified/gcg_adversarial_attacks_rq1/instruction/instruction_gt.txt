## Experimental Design

### 1. Transferability of Adversarial Prompts

- **Procedure**: 
  - Use the Greedy Coordinate Gradient (GCG) method to generate adversarial prompts optimized on open-source models (Vicuna-7B, Vicuna-13B, and Guanaco-7B).
  - Evaluate these prompts on proprietary black-box models such as GPT-3.5, GPT-4, and Claude.
  - Use the AdvBench dataset, which includes 500 harmful strings and 500 harmful behaviors, to test the effectiveness of the adversarial prompts.
  - For each harmful behavior, append the adversarial suffix to the user query and pass it to the model.
  - Conduct experiments with different configurations: single behavior on a single model, and multiple behaviors on multiple models.

- **What to Record**: 
  - Record the raw outputs of the models when prompted with adversarial inputs.
  - Extract the success rate of the model generating harmful content (Attack Success Rate, ASR).
  - Record the cross-entropy loss on the target string for harmful strings.

## Evaluation Details

- **Metric Definitions**:
  - **Attack Success Rate (ASR)**: The percentage of test cases where the model generates the exact harmful string or makes a reasonable attempt at executing the harmful behavior.
  - **Cross-Entropy Loss**: Used as a secondary metric for harmful strings, measuring the effectiveness of the attack in terms of the model's output probability distribution.

- **Normalization and Pre-processing**:
  - Ensure that the model outputs are tokenized consistently using the same tokenizer as used during the adversarial prompt optimization.
  - For non-deterministic models like PaLM-2, evaluate multiple completions and consider the attack successful if any completion elicits the target behavior.

- **Aggregation Strategy**:
  - Compute ASR per sample and aggregate across all samples to obtain the overall ASR for each model and configuration.
  - Compare ASR across different models and configurations to assess transferability.

## Implementation Notes

- **Key Pitfalls**:
  - The paper notes that excessive optimization steps can lead to overfitting on the source models, reducing transferability to target models. Monitor the optimization process to avoid this.
  - Ensure that the adversarial suffix does not exceed the token limit of the target models, as this can lead to incomplete or failed queries.

- **Special Prompt Templates**:
  - Use the phrase "Sure, here is how to [user query]" as a target sequence to induce affirmative responses from the models.
  - For ensemble attacks, concatenate multiple GCG prompts and use them as a single suffix to increase ASR.

- **Order of Operations**:
  - First, optimize the adversarial suffix on open-source models using GCG.
  - Then, evaluate the transferability of these prompts on proprietary models.
  - Finally, analyze the results to determine the effectiveness and transferability of the adversarial prompts.
