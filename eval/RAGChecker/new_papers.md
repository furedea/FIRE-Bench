| 1 | `reversal_curse` | If an LLM is trained on statements of the form 'A is B,' can it automatically generalize to answer questions requiring the reverse relationship 'B is A'? | LLMs trained on 'A is B' fail to generalize to 'B is A.' When finetuned on fictitious facts like 'Uriah Hawthorne is the composer of Abyssal Melodies,' models cannot answer the reverse. The likelihood of the correct answer is no higher than for a random name. Testing GPT-4 on real celebrity-parent pairs showed the same asymmetry across 1,000 pairs. | Berglund et al., 2023 |
| 2 | `llm_judge_position_bias` | When LLMs are used as evaluators to compare two candidate responses, does the order in which responses are presented systematically bias the evaluation outcome? | LLMs exhibit strong position bias when used as evaluators: quality rankings can be easily manipulated by altering response order. By simply swapping response order, Vicuna-13B appeared to beat ChatGPT on 66 out of 80 queries when ChatGPT was the evaluator. Calibration strategies including multiple evidence, balanced position, and human-in-the-loop mitigate this bias. | Wang et al., 2023 |
| 3 | `sycophancy_in_llms` | Do state-of-the-art AI assistants exhibit sycophantic behavior by tailoring responses to match user beliefs rather than being truthful, and what drives this behavior? | Five state-of-the-art AI assistants consistently exhibit sycophancy across four varied free-form text-generation tasks. Responses matching a user's views are more likely to be preferred, and both humans and preference models prefer convincingly-written sycophantic responses over correct ones a non-negligible fraction of the time. RLHF training partly drives this behavior. | Sharma et al., 2023 |
| 4 | `llm_reliability_scaling` | As language models are scaled up in size and shaped through instruction tuning and RLHF, do they become more reliable in correctly answering questions or avoiding errors? | Larger and more instructable LLMs become less reliable in important ways. Scaled-up models do not secure areas of low difficulty where errors can be caught by human supervision. Larger instruction-tuned models tend to give apparently sensible yet wrong answers much more often, including on difficult questions that human supervisors frequently overlook. | Zhou et al., 2024 (Nature) |
| 5 | `gsm_symbolic` | Do LLMs perform genuine mathematical reasoning, or are they relying on pattern matching, and how robust is their performance to superficial changes in math problems? | LLM performance declines when only numerical values in math problems are altered, revealing significant variance across different instantiations. Adding a single irrelevant-but-plausible clause causes performance drops of up to 65% across all state-of-the-art models, suggesting LLMs perform probabilistic pattern-matching rather than formal reasoning. | Mirzadeh et al., 2024 |
| 6 | `irrelevant_context_distraction` | How does the inclusion of irrelevant information in problem descriptions affect LLM accuracy on arithmetic reasoning tasks? | Model performance drops dramatically when irrelevant information is included, with fewer than 30% of base problems consistently solved after adding distractors. This persists across all prompting techniques. However, including irrelevant information within few-shot exemplars consistently boosts performance, and self-consistency decoding also helps. | Shi et al., 2023 |
| 7 | `inverse_scaling` | Are there tasks where larger language models perform worse than smaller ones, exhibiting inverse scaling? | Across 11 datasets, larger LMs show worse task performance on certain tasks. Four causes: preference to repeat memorized sequences, imitation of undesirable training data patterns, tasks containing an easy distractor sub-task, and correct but misleading few-shot demonstrations. U-shaped and inverted-U scaling trends were also revealed. | McKenzie et al., 2023 |
| 8 | `embers_of_autoregression` | Do LLMs show systematic performance differences based on the probability of the input, output, or task being performed, even in deterministic settings where probability should be irrelevant? | Across eleven tasks, LLMs achieve higher accuracy when the input, output, or task has higher probability in natural text, even for deterministic tasks. GPT-4's accuracy at decoding a simple shift cipher is 51% when the output is high-probability but only 13% when low-probability. Autoregressive training leaves persistent traces that shape model behavior. | McCoy et al., 2023 |
| 9 | `llm_error_finding` | Is the poor self-correction performance of LLMs due to an inability to find mistakes, an inability to correct them, or both? | Poor self-correction stems from LLMs' inability to locate logical mistakes, not from an inability to correct known mistakes. When provided with the ground-truth location of an error, LLMs can robustly correct mistakes across five reasoning tasks. A small classifier trained on out-of-domain data exhibits stronger mistake-finding performance than prompting a large model directly. | Tyen et al., 2024 |
| 10 | `cognitive_biases_in_llms` | Do LLMs exhibit human-like cognitive biases and intuitive reasoning errors, and how does this change with model scale and instruction tuning? | As models scale up, they increasingly display human-like System 1 thinking and cognitive errors. GPT-3 exhibits behavior resembling human intuition and its errors. However, ChatGPT learned to avoid these errors, performing in a hyperrational manner even when prevented from chain-of-thought reasoning. | Hagendorff et al., 2023 (Nature Computational Science) |
| 11 | `survey_response_biases` | Do LLMs exhibit the same response biases as humans when presented with survey questionnaires known to trigger specific biases in human respondents? | LLMs generally fail to reflect human-like survey response biases, particularly models that have undergone RLHF. Even when a model shows a statistically significant change in the same direction as humans, it is also sensitive to perturbations that do not elicit significant changes in humans. No monotonic trend between model size and bias magnitude. | Tjuatja et al., 2024 |
| 12 | `mt_bench_llm_judge` | How well do LLM judges agree with human preferences when evaluating model outputs, and what systematic biases do they exhibit? | Strong LLM judges like GPT-4 achieve over 80% agreement with human preferences, matching inter-human agreement. Three key biases identified: position bias (preferring responses in certain positions), verbosity bias (preferring longer responses), and self-enhancement bias (rating their own outputs higher). | Zheng et al., 2023 |
| 13 | `alice_in_wonderland` | Can state-of-the-art LLMs solve simple, short common-sense reasoning problems that are trivially easy for humans? | Most LLMs exhibit dramatic reasoning breakdown on the simple AIW problem (e.g., 'Alice has N sisters. How many sisters does Alice's brother have?'). Most models achieve below 0.2 correct response rate. Only GPT-4 and Claude 3 Opus were major exceptions. Models express strong overconfidence in wrong solutions, and CoT prompting fails to fix the errors. | Nezhurina et al., 2024 |
| 14 | `multilingual_cot_reasoning` | Can language models perform chain-of-thought reasoning effectively across diverse languages, including underrepresented ones, and how does this ability scale with model size? | CoT reasoning across languages emerges with model scale, with strong performance even in underrepresented languages like Bengali and Swahili. The 540B PaLM model solves more than 40% of problems in every tested language. No strong correlation between performance and language frequency in training data, suggesting cross-lingual knowledge transfer. | Shi et al., 2022 |
| 15 | `content_effects_on_reasoning` | Do LLMs, like humans, allow semantic content to influence their performance on logical reasoning tasks, performing better when content aligns with real-world knowledge? | LLMs reflect many human patterns on reasoning tasks. Like humans, models answer more accurately when semantic content supports logical inferences. On syllogisms, both show moderate accuracy with substantial content effects. However, on the Wason selection task, humans perform much worse than large models and exhibit a distinct error pattern. | Dasgupta et al., 2022 |
| 16 | `bbq_bias_benchmark` | Do NLP models rely on social stereotypes when answering questions, and does providing sufficient context eliminate this bias? | Models consistently rely on stereotypes when context is ambiguous. Even with adequate context, accuracy is on average 3.4 percentage points higher when the correct answer aligns with a social stereotype than when it conflicts, widening to over 5 points for gender-related examples. Bias persists even when models have sufficient information to answer correctly. | Parrish et al., 2022 |
| 17 | `compositionality_gap` | Can language models compose multiple facts to answer multi-hop questions, and does the compositionality gap decrease with scale? | As model size increases, single-hop QA improves faster than multi-hop performance, meaning the compositionality gap does not decrease with scale. Larger models memorize more facts but show no improvement in composing them. Self-ask, where the model explicitly asks and answers follow-up questions, improves compositional reasoning. | Press et al., 2023 |
| 18 | `faith_and_fate_compositionality` | Can transformer-based LLMs develop systematic problem-solving skills for compositional tasks, or do they rely on pattern matching from training data? | Transformers solve compositional tasks by reducing multi-step reasoning into linearized subgraph matching, without developing systematic problem-solving skills. Success is linked to having seen portions of the computation graph during training. Across multiplication, logic puzzles, and dynamic programming, performance declines from perfect to zero as complexity increases. | Dziri et al., 2023 |
| 19 | `llm_alternative_human_eval` | When LLMs are given the exact same evaluation instructions, samples, and questions used in human evaluation studies, do their assessments align with human expert judgments? | LLM evaluation results are consistent with expert human evaluation: texts rated higher by experts are also rated higher by LLMs. Results are stable across different formatting and sampling algorithms. However, LLMs exhibit factual misunderstandings and lack of sentiment understanding, making them most effective in combination with human evaluation. | Chiang & Lee, 2023 |
| 20 | `emergent_abilities_mirage` | Are the sharp, unpredictable emergent abilities observed in large language models a genuine property of scaling, or an artifact of how researchers measure performance? | Emergent abilities are primarily a mirage caused by metric choice. Nonlinear metrics like exact-match accuracy produce apparent emergence, while linear metrics like token-level log-likelihood reveal smooth improvement. Three factors explain it: metrics that nonlinearly deform per-token error rates, insufficient test data, and insufficient sampling of larger parameter regimes. | Schaeffer et al., 2023 |
